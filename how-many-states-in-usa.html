Governing body

The statement "Governing body: Federal government" in the context of "How Many States In Usa?" highlights the fundamental relationship between the federal government and the individual states that constitute the United States of America.

The United States is a federal republic, meaning that it is governed by a central government (the federal government) and by individual state governments. The federal government is responsible for matters that affect the entire nation, such as foreign policy, defense, and interstate commerce. State governments are responsible for matters that primarily affect their own citizens, such as education, law enforcement, and public health.

The division of power between the federal government and the state governments is enshrined in the U.S. Constitution. The Constitution establishes a system of checks and balances to ensure that no one branch of government becomes too powerful. For example, the federal government can pass laws, but the Supreme Court can declare them unconstitutional. Similarly, the states can pass laws, but the federal government can override them if they conflict with federal law.

The relationship between the federal government and the state governments is complex and ever-evolving. However, it is essential to understanding how the United States is governed. By understanding the role of the federal government in relation to the states, we can better appreciate the delicate balance of power that has made the United States a successful democracy for over two centuries.
